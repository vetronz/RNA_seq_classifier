K=4

The result of clustering into 4 clusters is shown below in Figure XYZ. The majority of cases assigned to cluster 1 are viral accounting for 35 of 68 cases in that cluster. Cluster 2 was predominantly bacterial with 28 of the 51 bacterial cases and 10 of the 42 probable bacterial cases assigned to it. Cluster 3 was more heterogenious with 20 viral cases, 21 unknowns 14 probable bacterials and just 5 bacterials. Cluster 4 was predominatly viral 30 viral cases out of 58 total cases assigned to the cluster.

In Figure XYZ we see the same pattern as we observed for the case with K=2 where by cluster 2 with a higher bacterial burden had higher inflammatory markers. To understand why the bacterial cases were separated between clusters I have plotted the inflammatory markers looking exclusively at the 51 bacterial cases in Figure XYZ. We can see from this that the bacterial cases appear to have been partitioned on the basis of the inflammatory markers with the more severe infections with higher inflamamatory markers being assigned to cluster 2.

Figure XYZ.1 and .2 show the organ system involvement for each cluster and the pathogenic organism detected. We see the same effect as was present in the K=2 case where by a higher proportion of bacteraemic cases were assigned to cluster 2. The causative pathogen in the majority of these cases was meningococcus as seen in Figure XYZ.2. However not all of the meningococcal cases were assigned to cluster 2. Clusters 1 and 4 both had a single meningococcal patient and cluster 3 had 2. To better understand why these cases were split from the main body of meningococcal cases who were assigned to cluster 2 I have presented Table XYZ below which details the clinical summary for all of the meningococcal patients. The K2_Cluster and K4_Cluster columns detail the cluster assignement for the k=2 and k=4 cases respectively. It is interesting to note that the outlier meningococcal cases 15, 16 and 17 that were the only cases assigned to cluster 2 have also been isolated to distinct clusters in the K=4 case with 2 assigned to cluster 3 and 1 assigned to its own cluster 4.

To visualize why these cases have been partitioned from the bulk of the meningococcal cases I have plotted in Figure XYZ a 2 dimensional PCA plot. The 4 colours indicate the cluster assignment and the crosses are meningococcal cases. We can see the bulk of the cases are relatively closely clusterd and have been assigned to cluster 2. However zooming in over the 0, 0 coordinate we can see the 2 meningococcal cases assigned to cluster 3 and the case assigned to cluster 4. It is likely that co-infection with viral or other pathogens have distorted the characteristic meningococcal transcriptomic signature and pulled these cases into other clusters.
